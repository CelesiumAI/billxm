Our Story

Built by a 15-year-old and his 14-year-old brother who couldn't let their grandmother's $751,487 bill go unquestioned

In early September 2025, our grandmother fell and broke her hip. She was admitted to Memorial Hermann Hospital, one of Houston's largest hospital systems — and then readmitted multiple times as complications arose from the surgery.

With each admission came another massive bill. Five admissions. Sixty-six pages. $751,486.81 in total charges. Our family stared at these bills and had the same question millions of families have: Is this right? How would we even know?

So we decided to find out. Ian, a 15-year-old sophomore, and Grayson, his 14-year-old brother in eighth grade, used coding skills learned from their mentor David Zhang to build a program that reads every line item and compares it against the U.S. government's official published rates for every medical procedure. What we found was staggering — $323,044 in overcharges, including ECG tracings marked up 16,000% and blood tests billed at 80× the federal rate.

That's how BillXM was born — built by two teenagers who refused to accept that a medical bill is correct just because it's big.

Memorial Hermann Billing Department

Call: 713-338-5502  ·  Based on 30 verified overcharges  ·  $323,044 in dispute

Step 1 — Opening Statement
"Hello, I'm calling about a recent bill totaling $751,486.81 across five hospital admissions. I've had it professionally reviewed and found 30 billing issues I'd like to discuss. May I have your name and a reference number for this call?"
Get a name and reference number before you say anything else. Write it down.
Step 2 — Largest Overcharge · Lab Panels · Potential savings: $30,391
"The charge of $30,780 for code 80048 — basic metabolic panels — has a Medicare rate of $8.46 per test, or $389 total for 46 panels. That's a 7,800% markup. I'd like to discuss this charge and what adjustments are available."
The government's Clinical Lab Fee Schedule is national and publicly available. This is your strongest factual argument.
Step 3 — CT Scans · Potential savings: $28,552
"Three CT scans of my abdomen and pelvis were billed at $9,478 to $9,987 each — code 74177 — totaling $29,453. Medicare's published facility rate is $300.38 per scan. That's a 3,224% markup. I'm requesting an adjustment to reflect the published Medicare rate."
CT scans have a clearly published government rate. Billing departments know this. They expect this call.
Step 4 — ECG Tracings · Potential savings: $24,704
"Twenty-four ECG tracings were billed at $1,039 each under code 93005, totaling $24,855. Medicare's facility rate for an ECG tracing is $6.30 — that is a 16,000% markup. I'm disputing this as excessive and requesting adjustment."
16,000% is the most dramatic number in the report. Say it calmly. Let it land.
Step 5 — ER Visit Severity · Potential savings: $21,110
"All four of my emergency visits were billed at Level 5 — the highest severity — code 99285, at $5,298 to $5,510 per visit. Medicare's rate for a Level 5 ER visit is $176.43. I'd like to understand what clinical criteria justified Level 5 for each visit, and I'm requesting a clinical review of the severity coding."
Requesting a clinical review puts the burden on them to justify Level 5 coding with documentation.
Step 6 — Possible Upcoding · Catheter · Potential savings: $16,530
"There are two charges billed under code 36410 — venipuncture — at $8,727 and $7,820. Code 36410 is for basic venipuncture with a Medicare rate of $9.09, but the services appear to be midline catheter insertions. I'm requesting a review for possible miscoding — this is over $16,500 in charges."
The word "miscoding" signals you know what you're looking at. It's a compliance term, not an accusation.
